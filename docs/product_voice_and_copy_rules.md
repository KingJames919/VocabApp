# Product Voice and Copy Rules (V1)

## 1. Purpose

### What this document is for
- This document defines how all learner-facing language inside the app should be written.
- It creates one consistent voice across instructions, feedback, transitions, progress language, and recap language.
- It is meant to keep product language stable as the app grows.
- It is a writing standard, not a content plan, not a UI spec, and not an implementation guide.

### Why voice matters in this product
- This product asks learners to do real thinking: read carefully, notice meaning, compare words, write briefly, and recall what matters.
- The language of the product should support that work without distracting from it.
- Voice is part of the learning experience. It shapes how effort feels, how feedback lands, and whether the product feels calm, useful, and trustworthy.
- Because the product is grounded in vocabulary growth, knowledge building, morphology, retrieval, and transfer, the language should reinforce clarity, connection, and cumulative growth rather than noise, hype, or empty reward language.

---

## 2. Core Voice Principles

### 1. Say what matters
- Lead with the learning task.
- Use direct language.
- Put the key idea early.

### 2. Be warm, not gushy
- Sound supportive without sounding performative.
- Avoid praise that says nothing.
- Respect the learner’s attention.

### 3. Make meaning visible
- Point to what the learner is noticing, choosing, building, using, or recalling.
- Frame tasks around understanding, not just completion.

### 4. Keep language precise
- Use specific verbs.
- Name the exact problem when feedback is needed.
- Prefer concrete wording over broad encouragement.

### 5. Reduce noise
- Keep sentences short.
- Avoid extra commentary.
- Do not stack multiple ideas when one clear sentence will do.

### 6. Help the learner move forward
- Good copy should tell the learner what to do next, what got stronger, or what needs revision.
- It should never leave the learner with a vague feeling of success or failure.

## 3. Tone Definition

### The tone is:
- warm and encouraging
- calm and clear
- purposeful
- steady
- respectful
- never noisy

### Warm and encouraging means:
- the copy sounds human
- it assumes the learner can improve
- it gives support without overpraising
- it treats struggle as normal and useful

**In practice:**
- “Try that idea again with a more exact word.”
- “You’re close. Pick the word that best fits the evidence.”
- “This sentence needs a clearer job for *pressure*.”

### Calm and clear means:
- no hype
- no fake excitement
- no crowded instructions
- no lecture tone
- no extra decoration in the wording

**In practice:**
- “Read the passage once all the way through.”
- “Build the word, then check what it means.”
- “Choose the word that fits both the sentence and the idea.”

### Tone guardrails
- The voice should feel more like a thoughtful guide than a performer.
- It should feel more like a skilled coach than a cheerleader.
- It should feel more like a learning environment than an app trying to entertain.

## 4. Subtle Growth Language Layer

### What this layer is
- The product uses a quiet sense of growth and connection in its language.
- This is not a visible theme. It is a background pattern in wording.
- When it fits naturally, the language may suggest:
  - ideas taking hold
  - words becoming more stable
  - connections forming across words and meanings
  - understanding building over time

### How to use it
- Use this language sparingly.
- Never force it.
- Prefer natural phrasing over metaphor-heavy wording.
- Keep it light enough that it feels almost invisible.
- Use it to support meaning, not to create a theme.

### What it should sound like
- subtle
- natural
- grounded in learning
- calm, not decorative

### What to avoid
- language that sounds like a visible theme
- repeated plant or growth metaphors
- anything that sounds like a game, quest, or reward system
- lines that draw attention to the wording itself

### Examples
- “This word is starting to take hold.”
- “This idea is becoming clearer.”
- “You’re seeing how these words connect.”
- “This pattern shows up across related words.”

## 5. How We Talk to the Learner

### How instructions should sound
- direct
- short
- action-first
- easy to follow in one read
- focused on one task at a time

**Instruction rules**
- Start with the action.
- Name the goal when helpful.
- Avoid stacking too many steps in one sentence.
- Prefer everyday words unless a domain term is necessary.

**Examples**
- “Read the passage and notice what changes before a storm.”
- “Build the word, then choose the best meaning.”
- “Write one sentence that uses *predict* clearly.”

### How feedback should sound
- specific
- brief
- honest
- useful for revision
- tied to meaning, not just correctness

**Feedback rules**
- Say what worked only when it is real and specific.
- Say what needs fixing in plain language.
- Point to the word, idea, or sentence that needs attention.
- Give the learner a next move.

**Examples**
- “You used *predict* correctly.”
- “*Pressure* is related, but here it is too vague.”
- “This answer fits the topic, but it does not explain the cause clearly.”

### How transitions should sound
- light
- grounding
- cumulative
- never dramatic

**Transition rules**
- Show how the next step builds on the last one.
- Keep transitions short.
- Reinforce continuity across the five-session sequence.

**Examples**
- “Now you’ll work with the word family behind *transport*.”
- “Next, you’ll choose between close word meanings.”
- “Now use the words to explain the weather idea more clearly.”

## 6. What Good Copy Looks Like

### Strong instructions
- “Read the passage once before you look closely at the words.”
- “Choose the word that best matches the meaning in this sentence.”
- “Use two target words to explain how meteorologists study storms.”
- “Look for the part of the word that carries meaning across the family.”

### Strong feedback
- “This choice matches the topic, but not the exact meaning.”
- “You used *severe* correctly. It shows the storm is serious, not just large.”
- “This sentence is on topic, but the family connection is weak.”
- “Try revising this sentence so the word does more precise work.”

### Strong learning framing
- “These words work together to explain the topic.”
- “This family can help you make sense of new words.”
- “You are not just recognizing the word. You are using it to explain an idea.”
- “Review helps make the word easier to use later.”
- “You’re starting to see how these words connect.”
- “This pattern shows up across related words.”

## 7. Forbidden Language Patterns

### 1. Generic praise

**Why it is not allowed**
- It adds noise.
- It does not help learning.
- It rewards completion without naming what improved.

**Bad example**
- “Great job!”

**Better version**
- “You used *predict* accurately.”
- “That answer matches the evidence in the passage.”

---

### 2. Vague instructions

**Why it is not allowed**
- It makes the task harder than it needs to be.
- It forces the learner to guess what to do.

**Bad example**
- “Think carefully and do your best.”

**Better version**
- “Choose the word that best fits the sentence.”
- “Revise your response so both required words are used clearly.”

---

### 3. System or debug language

**Why it is not allowed**
- It breaks the learning experience.
- It makes the product sound mechanical.
- It shifts attention away from meaning.

**Bad example**
- “Response submitted successfully.”
- “You triggered a revision state.”
- “Incorrect input detected.”

**Better version**
- “Your response is saved.”
- “You can revise once.”
- “That word does not fit this sentence.”

---

### 4. Over-explanation

**Why it is not allowed**
- It slows the learner down.
- It crowds the screen with language the learner does not need.
- It weakens the force of the main instruction or feedback.

**Bad example**
- “In this next activity, you will have an opportunity to demonstrate your developing understanding of how words can relate to one another within a larger morphological structure.”

**Better version**
- “Now build the word family.”
- “Look at how these words connect.”

---

### 5. Robotic tone

**Why it is not allowed**
- It feels cold and unnatural.
- It reduces trust.
- It makes feedback harder to absorb.

**Bad example**
- “The selected response does not satisfy task criteria.”

**Better version**
- “This answer does not match the meaning closely enough.”

---

### 6. Gamified or noisy reward language

**Why it is not allowed**
- It clashes with the product’s calm, content-first experience.
- It shifts motivation toward reward instead of understanding.
- It makes real progress harder to see.

**Bad example**
- “Nice! You crushed it!”
- “Level up!”
- “You earned a bonus!”
- “Streak preserved!”

**Better version**
- “This word is starting to take hold.”
- “You handled this word well in a new context.”
- “This pattern is becoming clearer across related words.”

---

### 7. Empty reassurance

**Why it is not allowed**
- It sounds kind, but it does not help.
- It can blur the difference between a strong answer and a weak one.

**Bad example**
- “That’s okay! You’re doing amazing!”

**Better version**
- “This idea is on the right track, but the word choice is not precise yet.”
- “Try again with a word that better matches the evidence.”

---

### 8. Teacher-lecture wording

**Why it is not allowed**
- It sounds formal and distant.
- It turns learner-facing copy into explanation-heavy instruction.
- It adds friction where the app should feel light and direct.

**Bad example**
- “Students should now demonstrate comprehension of the semantic distinction among near-synonymous lexical items.”

**Better version**
- “Now choose between close word meanings.”

---

## 8. Progress Language Guidance

### What progress language should do
- show that understanding is building
- connect progress to word use and meaning
- reinforce that learning is cumulative
- reflect evidence, not reward

### What progress language should not do
- use points
- use streaks
- use badges as the main signal
- use generic reward language
- imply permanent mastery from one success

### Progress should be tied to:
- clearer understanding
- stronger word use
- more accurate explanation
- better recall
- better transfer to a new example
- stronger connections across a word family

### Good progress language patterns
- “This word is starting to take hold.”
- “This word is becoming more stable.”
- “You used this word more precisely here.”
- “This idea is becoming clearer.”
- “You’re seeing how this connects across words.”
- “You recognized the family clue in a new word.”
- “You’re seeing this connection across more examples.”
- “This part still needs review.”

### Progress language rules
- Keep it evidence-based.
- Do not exaggerate.
- Do not make every success sound major.
- Do not describe completion as growth unless growth is actually shown.
- Mix direct progress language with lighter growth language when it fits.
- Prefer phrases like “becoming clearer,” “more precise,” “starting to take hold,” and “needs review” over inflated claims.
- For Final Recap status, prefer `Steady for now` and `Needs another look`. Keep `solid`, `fragile`, and transfer-state values internal. Use `related word` or `new related word` for learner-facing morphology transfer moments.

### Progress framing by session type
- **After reading:** focus on what the learner now notices or understands.
- **After morphology work:** focus on connection and transfer.
- **After precision work:** focus on exact fit and clearer meaning.
- **After writing:** focus on clarity, accuracy, and stronger use.
- **After review:** focus on what held, what transferred, and what still needs support.

## 9. Writing Constraints

### Sentence length
- Prefer short sentences.
- Most learner-facing sentences should be 6–14 words.
- Longer sentences are allowed only when clarity requires them.
- Avoid multi-clause instructions when a simpler version will work.

### Clarity expectations
- The learner should understand the instruction on the first read.
- Feedback should identify the issue quickly.
- Every sentence should earn its place.

### When to simplify
- Simplify when the task is procedural.
- Simplify when the learner needs to act immediately.
- Simplify when the same idea can be said with fewer words.
- Simplify when a sentence includes more than one new idea.

### When to be explicit
- Be explicit when naming what is wrong.
- Be explicit when distinguishing close meanings.
- Be explicit when the learner must revise something specific.
- Be explicit when the task depends on precision.

### Practical rules
- One main action per sentence.
- One main idea per sentence when possible.
- Prefer concrete verbs: read, choose, build, revise, explain, recall.
- Avoid filler phrases.
- Avoid softening language that hides the point.
- Avoid rhetorical questions unless there is a strong reason to use one.
- Avoid exclamation points in normal instructional copy.
- Avoid obvious, repeated, or decorative metaphors.
- If growth language is used, it should feel natural and almost invisible.
- Use emphasis sparingly and only when it supports meaning.

---

## 10. Non-Goals

This voice should **not** try to be:

### A teacher lecture
- It should not over-explain.
- It should not sound formal or instructional in a classroom-script way.
- It should guide action, not perform teaching monologues.

### A game narrator
- It should not hype every step.
- It should not turn tasks into quests, wins, or rewards.
- It should not narrate progress like entertainment.

### Overly academic
- It should not sound dense, abstract, or expert-facing.
- It should not use unnecessary educational jargon.
- It should not make the learner decode the interface language.

### Overly casual
- It should not sound slangy, flippant, or winky.
- It should not try to sound trendy.
- It should not sacrifice precision for friendliness.

### A motivational poster
- It should not lean on slogans.
- It should not use empty positivity.
- It should not promise confidence when the learner needs clarity.

---

## Final Standard

All learner-facing copy in V1 should feel like this:

- clear enough to follow immediately
- calm enough to support concentration
- warm enough to reduce friction
- precise enough to improve learning
- grounded in words, meaning, knowledge, and transfer
- quiet enough to let the learning do the work
